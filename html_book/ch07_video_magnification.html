Chapter 7

Video Magnification

Some motions are too small to see: a pulse in the neck, a building swaying in the wind, an engine's vibration. Eulerian video processing amplifies them with a one-line Taylor trick — no per-pixel motion estimation needed.

7   Video Magnification

Original text (PDF pages 50–51)

Following the previous section, motion magnification (also called Lagrangian Video Processing) estimates the motion vector of each pixel, clusters the pixels into layers, and amplifies the motion in a specific layer.

However, a new approach, called Eulerian Video Processing, addresses the same problem in a simpler and more elegant way. The main difference between the two approaches lies in the coordinates: Lagrangian methods track individual pixels over time, whereas Eulerian methods fixate on a region in the frame and observe its intensity profile. In other words, Lagrangian methods operate in the image plane, while Eulerian methods operate in the time domain. In the Eulerian approach, each pixel is observed independently, without considering its neighborhood. The most distinctive property of the Eulerian method is its ability to capture subtle, minor changes.

Given I(x, t) a 1-D image, where I(x, t) is the value of the piel at time t. We will define:

$$ I(x, t) = f(x + \delta(t)), \qquad I(x, 0) = f(x) $$

Intuitively, at every pixel we observe: [intensity profile]

Our goal is to calculate:

$$ \tilde{I}(x, t) = f(x + (1 + \alpha)\delta(t)) $$

Here, $\alpha$ is a given amplification factor. There are two approaches to motion magnification. The first is the Lagrangian approach, which operates in the image plane. The second is the Eulerian approach, which observes the temporal derivative. This method links the rate of change over time to motion in the image plane: if a pixel moves quickly in the image plane, its temporal derivative will be large.

For that, we will use first order Tailor approximation:

$$ I(x, t) \approx f(x) + \underbrace{\delta(t) \frac{\partial f(x)}{\partial x}}_{B(x,t)} $$

We want: $\tilde{I}(x, t) = I(x.t) + \alpha B(x, t)$. We insert:

$$ \tilde{I}(x, t) \approx f(x) + (1 + \alpha)\delta(t) \frac{\partial f(x)}{\partial x} $$

And get: $\tilde{I}(x, t) \approx f(x + (1 + \alpha)\delta(t))$.

Plain-English explanation

Goal: reveal hidden motions — a pulse in the neck, a tall building's wind sway. Two approaches, named after fluid dynamics:

  • Lagrangian: follow each pixel ("particle") as it moves. Needs motion estimation.
  • Eulerian: sit at a fixed location and watch how the value changes over time. No motion estimation — just temporal filtering. Way simpler.

The Eulerian trick. By Taylor, $I(x,t) \approx f(x) + \delta(t) f'(x)$. The change-over- time term is $B(x,t) = \delta(t) f'(x)$. Add $\alpha$ copies of $B$ back to the original and you effectively have $(1+\alpha)\delta(t)$ — the motion has been amplified by $\alpha$.

In practice: compute $B \approx I_2 - I_1$, multiply by $\alpha$, add to $I_1$. Done.

Why the Taylor trick works

If something shifted by a tiny δ in space, the change in pixel value is approximately δ × slope. Multiply that change by α and add it back, and the pixel "moves" as if δ were (1+α)δ. Crucially, we never had to estimate δ explicitly. The temporal difference $I_2 - I_1$ IS the change-in-value we needed.

Why "sub-pixel" magnification is possible

A pixel value can encode a 0.01-pixel motion via an intensity change of 0.01 × slope — a small but real number. LK can't see that motion (its Taylor approximation is overwhelmed by sensor noise) but Eulerian magnification can amplify it 100× and make it visible. The motion was always in the data; we just couldn't perceive it.

Band-pass filtering — the missing ingredient

Real implementations don't amplify ALL temporal changes — that would amplify lighting drift, JPEG noise, everything. Instead they band-pass the temporal signal first (e.g., keep only 1–2 Hz for heart-rate) and amplify just that band. The result: heart rate visualized, ambient lighting unchanged.

Why this matters in real life

  • Medicine: contactless pulse and respiration measurement from a webcam.
  • Civil engineering: measure micro-vibrations of buildings, bridges, dams without attaching sensors.
  • Manufacturing: diagnose machine faults from camera footage of housings.
  • Music: reconstruct sound from video of a chip bag vibrating ("the visual microphone").

Limits of the trick

If the motion exceeds about 1 pixel, the Taylor approximation breaks and you get ringing/ghosting. Solution again: pyramid decomposition + amplify each spatial scale independently.

Rule of Thumb
  • Pick Eulerian over Lagrangian for sub-pixel and low-amplitude motion. Eulerian is also orders of magnitude simpler to implement.
  • α between 5 and 50 is the typical useful range. Higher α reveals more but introduces more artifacts (ringing).
  • Temporally band-pass first if you know the target frequency (heartrate ≈ 1 Hz, respiration ≈ 0.2 Hz, building sway ≈ 0.5 Hz). Cuts noise dramatically.
  • If you see ringing/halos around edges, your motion exceeded 1 pixel — switch to pyramid magnification (amplify each scale separately).
  • Keep the camera rock-still. Any camera shake gets magnified too — mount it on a tripod.
